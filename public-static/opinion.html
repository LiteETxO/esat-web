Ethiopia's federal experiment: can unity survive a decade of reform?

✦
Dr. Aster Woldemichael Contributing Analyst · Political Science, Addis Ababa University

Ethiopia's 1995 constitution created one of the world's most ambitious experiments in ethnic federalism. It gave nations, nationalities, and peoples the explicit right to self-determination — including, in theory, secession. What it could not give them was a political culture to match those rights.

A decade of reform since 2018 has tested every assumption embedded in that document. New regional boundaries have been drawn and contested. Federal forces have deployed against regional militias. A civil war consumed the north. And yet the federation holds — not comfortably, and not without cost, but it holds.

The question for the next decade is not whether Ethiopia will remain one country. It almost certainly will. The question is what kind of country it chooses to be: one where federalism is a lived reality, or one where it remains a constitutional decoration over a centralised state.
